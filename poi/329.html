• Food (1 of 3)

    Each enslaved adult on Washington’s farms received a weekly distribution that provided a daily ration of about 4 cups of cornmeal and a few ounces of salted dried herring. These basic rations provided a small amount of protein and carbohydrates, but lacked essential nutrients and variety. Special fare, such as wheat flour, pork and beef, or rum, might be distributed at butchering, for holidays, or as a reward.
  • Supplementing Rations (2 of 3)

    To supplement their diets, enslaved men used their limited personal time to catch wildlife. After dark, they set box traps for squirrels, rabbits, raccoons, and opossums. Lead shot found in excavations confirms some slaves had access to firearms to hunt deer and ducks. They also crafted fishing hooks from bone or metal and sometimes borrowed Washington’s large nets, called seines, to catch fish. More than 50 species of wild animals have been identified archaeologically.
  • Selling Food (3 of 3)

    Enslaved people could earn income by selling food and goods to the Washingtons or at the market in Alexandria. In addition to fish, duck, and game, enslaved men sold Washington honey, melons, and brooms, purchasing in return special food and drink. The men at Mansion House Farm may have grown produce themselves, or received it from their families on the outlying farms.
