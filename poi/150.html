• Illustrious Personages (1 of 3)

    These three pictures are from Charles Willson Peale’s series of mezzotints based on his portraits of “illustrious personages.” On July 3, 1787, while George Washington was in Philadelphia presiding over the Constitutional Convention, he recorded in his diary, “Sat before the meeting of the Convention for Mr. Peale, who wanted my picture to make a print of Metzotinto by.” Washington sat for Peale twice more in the following week.

    Purchase, 1989 [M-2086/A]
  • Marquis de Lafayette (2 of 3)

    In September 1787, Peale sent some of the completed mezzotints to Washington, who eventually hung three of them—featuring himself, the Marquis de Lafayette, and Benjamin Franklin—in the little parlor. The location of Washington’s original prints are unknown; these three images are photographic facsimiles of period examples.

    Purchase, 1989 [M-2086/B]
  • Benjamin Franklin (3 of 3)

    Peale’s mezzotint of Benjamin Franklin was the first in his series, though the artist was unhappy with the finished product. In a letter to Washington on February 27, 1787, Peale writes, “I have finished a Plate of Doctr Franklin, which I give to the Public as a specimen of the Size and manner of my intended series of Prints, yet I do myself injustice, as this Print is much coarser than the others will be.”

    Purchase, 1989 [M-2086/C]
