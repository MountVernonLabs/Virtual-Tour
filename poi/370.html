• From Old Tomb to New Tomb (1 of 3)

    At the time of Washington’s death on December 14, 1799, George Washington was buried in the original family tomb at Mount Vernon, until a new one could be completed according to his specifications. After its completion in 1831, George Washington, Martha Washington, and the rest of the family members buried in the Old Tomb were reinterred in the new mausoleum, where they remain to this day.

  • Building the New Tomb (2 of 3)

    The New Tomb was built 32 years after George Washington’s death, and fulfilled one of his last wishes. His will outlined his instructions for a new tomb that could replace the rapidly deteriorating family vault at Mount Vernon. Twelve of Washington’s formerly enslaved workers and their descendants returned to Mount Vernon to work as volunteers on the construction and landscaping, which was completed in 1831. The vault into which the remains of the Washingtons and other family members were moved was not yet in its final form. It was a simple brick structure entered through a square iron doorway which is still visible through the gates of the tomb.

  • Capitol Tomb (3 of 3)

    After Washington’s death, Congress petitioned Martha Washington to allow her husband’s remains to be buried in a vault under the Capitol dome. Although Martha gave her consent and a crypt was provided, the project stalled. By 1831, the new tomb at Mount Vernon was completed, and his remains were moved there. The centennial of Washington’s birth in 1832 reignited Congressional interest in the Capitol tomb, and a request was again made to the Washington family. Washington’s great-nephew and then-proprietor of Mount Vernon, John A. Washington II, refused the request, honoring Washington’s implicit wishes to be buried at Mount Vernon. The completed vault under the Capitol rotunda remains empty to this day.
