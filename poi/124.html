• The New Room in Time (1 of 5)

    The earliest known view of Mount Vernon’s New Room appeared in 1849 in the popular magazine, Godey’s Lady’s Book. Although the Mansion was still owned by the Washington family, the New Room was regularly visited by curious sightseers, as shown here. Washington’s carved marble mantel and plaster ceiling ornament are clearly visible, but a flowered carpet covers the original wood floor and the Palladian window is swathed in heavy draperies.
  • The New Room in Time (2 of 5)

    From 1873 until 1902, the New Room was dominated by an 11-foot tall equestrian portrait of Washington Before Yorktown, painted in the 1820s by Rembrandt Peale and donated to Mount Vernon by the artist’s daughter. A number of original objects were also on view in the room: two silver-plated Argand lamps above the mantel, and two rococo candle stands on the hearth, accompanied by Washington’s surveyor’s tripod.

    Postcard, ca. 1899.
  • The New Room in Time (3 of 5)

    In 1932, the bicentennial of George Washington’s birth prompted a refurbishing of the New Room (then called the Banquet Hall), carried out by the New York interiors firm of French and Company. An “exquisite pale green” wall paint replaced the previous buff color, and the windows were hung with draperies made from 18th-century silk damask. The early 19th-century carpet displays the Great Seal of the United States.

    Postcard, 1932.
  • The New Room in Time (4 of 5)

    This 1958 photograph shows the re-introduction of wallpaper, based on 18th-century fragments found in the New Room. The green color prompted strong reactions, as the estate’s superintendent commented, “We are not in accord with the General’s taste in colors.” The new window hangings, consisting of green silk swags over white under curtains, were based on a 1799 visitor’s account that had come to light in 1947.

  • The New Room in Time (5 of 5)

    The early 1980s witnessed a new restoration campaign, as Mount Vernon turned to scientific paint analysis to reassess the colors used throughout the Mansion. The New Room’s green wall color deepened, with a green background on the ceiling cove. By 1988, furnishings in the room had been brought closer to those listed in Washington’s probate inventory, with the installation of matching sideboards, large gilt-framed looking glasses, and neoclassical urn-back chairs.
