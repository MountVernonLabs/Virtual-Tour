• The Washington Family (1 of 3)

    In the 1790s, Edward Savage completed a widely-admired portrait depicting the president, Martha Washington, and her grandchildren Eleanor (Nelly) and George Washington (Washy) Parke Custis looking at a plan for the new Federal City, to be named for the patriarch.

    Image credit: The Washington Family by Edward Savage, 1789-1796. Andrew W. Mellon Collection, National Gallery of Art, Washington, D.C. [1940.1.2]
  • A Rare Colored Version (2 of 3)

    Washington admired Savage's portrait so much that in 1798 he ordered from the artist four of the resulting stipple engravings in "handsome, but not costly, gilt frames, with glasses." Savage later sent a fifth engraving printed in color, seen here in its original frame and églomisé, as a gift to Martha Washington. One of these framed prints adorned the dining room at Mount Vernon.

    Gift of the Robert E. Wright Family, in memory of Dorothy Walton Wright and Robert Edward Wright, 2012 [W-5298]

  • Images of the Enslaved (3 of 3)

    Savage's portrait of the Washington family also includes an enslaved man wearing a red and white wool livery suit, the standard uniform for male slaves working in Washington’s household. This may be a depiction of Christopher Sheels, who served as Washington's valet during and after the presidency. The inclusion of a slave in the background was a common convention in 18th-century portraiture, indicating the wealth and elite status of the sitter.
