• Nelly’s Artwork (1 of 5)

    Like many elite young ladies of the period, Nelly Custis took lessons in drawing and embroidery, and her work was often displayed as evidence of her accomplishments. Facsimiles of five watercolors painted by Nelly hang on the wall of the little parlor. This one depicts a rural cottage, while others feature birds, a marine scene, and a memorial.

    Gift of Lyttleton B.P. Gould, Jr. and Family, 2002 [W-4246]
  • Nelly’s Paint Box (2 of 5)

    Nelly may have created these delicate watercolors using this paint box, which Washington purchased for her when the family was living in Philadelphia. Manufactured by London artist supply company Thomas Reeves and Sons, the well-equipped watercolor set featured 40 cakes of paint, ivory and ceramic mixing palettes, an ivory brush holder, and glass bowls for mixing colors, all nestled into individual compartments.

    Gift: Jess and Grace Pavey Fund, 2013 [W-5326/A-U]
    Image credit: Mark Finkenstaedt
  • Memorial to Fanny Bassett (3 of 5)

    This watercolor memorial expressed the Washington family’s grief when Fanny Bassett died in 1796, at age 28, of the same disease—tuberculosis—that had killed her first husband, George Augustine Washington, in 1793. She had subsequently married Tobias Lear, one of the president’s assistants. The watercolor, attributed to Nelly, is believed to depict the artist with her dog, Frisk, at Fanny’s tomb.

    Purchase, 1991 [W-3614/A-D]
