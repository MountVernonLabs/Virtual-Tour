• South Grove Midden (1 of 3)

    It was common practice in Washington’s day to dispose of household refuse near where it was generated, and out-of-the-way spots near the back doors of kitchens were particularly popular dumping grounds. Excavated in the 1990s by Mount Vernon's professional archaeologists, the south grove midden served as the primary site for Washington household related trash in the decades before the Revolutionary War.
  • Trunk Plate (2 of 3)

    One of the most remarkable objects recovered from the south grove midden was an oval copper trunk plate engraved with “Gen. Washington.” This recovered object is identical to a plate affixed to a trunk in the Mount Vernon collection which George Washington is known to have purchased secondhand on April 4, 1776, in Boston, soon after he took up his duties as general of the Continental Army.
  • Figurines (3 of 3)

    This incomplete artifact is a small clay figurine measuring only 2.8 inches (or 70.7 mm) without her head. This figurine is a woman with a small sheep or lamb seated at her feet, which is why she is often interpreted as a shepherdess. She was made in a two-piece mold, as the mold seams on either side of her body indicate, from white ball clay, the same material used to make tobacco pipes and wig curlers. Her male counterpart was also discarded in the midden, perhaps after he lost his head, too. While pipe clay figurines are not uncommon on archaeological sites in Virginia and Maryland, we have yet to find a shepherdess like this one.
