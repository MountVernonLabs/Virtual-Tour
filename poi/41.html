• Portraits of the Grandchildren (1 of 5)

    In the Washingtons’ bedchamber, where Mrs. Washington spent much of her day, she proudly displayed portraits of her four grandchildren. Seen here is the second-oldest granddaughter, Martha (Patty) Parke Custis (1777–1854), who was 7 years old when painted by the English artist Robert Edge Pine. Patty wears a miniature portrait that may depict her late father, John (Jack) Parke Custis (1754–1781).

  • Nelly Parke Custis (2 of 5)

    Martha’s youngest granddaughter, Eleanor (Nelly) Parke Custis (1779–1831) was only 5 years old when she sat for this portrait in 1785. X-ray examination of the portrait reveals that the head was painted on a separate, small piece of canvas, which was then pieced into the larger canvas. This method allowed the artist, Robert Edge Pine, to take a likeness quickly, then complete the portrait at his studio.

    Bequest of Marie Worthington Conrad Lehr in memory of her brother, Charles A. Conrad, 1922 [W-78]

  • Eliza Parke Custis (3 of 5)

    Elizabeth (Eliza) Parke Custis (1776–1831), Martha Washington’s oldest granddaughter, was 8 years old when painted by English artist Robert Edge Pine. She wears a miniature portrait depicting her father, John (Jack) Parke Custis (1754–1781), who died of a fever a few days after the Battle of Yorktown in October 1781. The canvas displayed in the bedchamber is a modern copy of the original portrait.

  • George Washington Parke Custis (4 of 5)

    This charming portrait by English artist Robert Edge Pine depicts Martha Washington’s only grandson, George Washington (Washy) Parke Custis (1781–1857) at age 4. The young lad wears an elegant light blue silk suit with military-style braiding and tassels and sleeves slashed to show his white shirt. The canvas currently hanging in the room is a modern copy of the 1785 original.

    Purchase, 1981 [M-2794]
    Image credit: Ted Vaughan
  • Blue Boy (5 of 5)

    Washy’s blue silk suit recalls that seen in Blue Boy, a celebrated portrait painted in 1770 by the English artist Thomas Gainsborough. This type of fancy dress (known as “Van Dyck” dress after the 17th-century painter, Antony Van Dyck) was popular among the upper classes in late 18th-century England. It would certainly have been familiar to Robert Edge Pine, whether or not young Washy Custis actually owned such an outfit.
