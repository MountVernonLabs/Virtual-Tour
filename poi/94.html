• Stove (1 of 4)

    This cast-iron stove, known as a Franklin fireplace, was made in Pennsylvania in the 1790s. Invented by Benjamin Franklin in the 1740s, such stoves introduced important advances in heating technology. Because the fire burned in a free-standing iron box, rather than a semi-enclosed fireplace, more heat radiated into the room. An internal flue in the back of the stove also drew air in and heated it by convection.

    Purchased with funds partially provided by Roland Kushmore, 1986 [M-3032]
  • Stove (2 of 4)

    The neoclassical decoration on the front of this stove—a central urn, curving arabesques, and beaded edges—would have made it a stylish addition to any American household in the 1790s. This example represents a similar type of stove that was installed by George Washington in 1797. Eighteenth-century Franklin stoves are now quite rare, and the original from this room does not survive.
  • Stove (3 of 4)

    As the end of summer in 1797 brought the expectation of cooler weather, George Washington asked Philadelphia merchant Clement Biddle to acquire for him, “an open stove for a room that has no fire place in it.” In this drawing, Washington showed the precise angle at which the stove pipe needed to be positioned to enter the chimney wall.

  • Stove (4 of 4)

    Only one of the garret chambers contains traces of a stovepipe hole, confirming the location of the 1797 stove installation. This room had previously had no fireplace and thus no source of heat in cold months. The addition of the stove was likely motivated by the need for a new sleeping chamber, to replace the first floor bedroom that was converted to the little parlor at about the same time.
