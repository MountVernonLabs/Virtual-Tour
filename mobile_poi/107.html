• Agricultural Motifs (1 of 3)

    When George Washington returned from the American Revolution, he was immediately identified as a modern day Cincinnatus after the Roman statesman, Lucius Quintus Cincinnatus (519-430 BC).  Cincinnatus fought to protect the Roman republic, and like Washington, after claiming victory returned to the plow of the farmer rather than assume absolute authority over the state.  Samuel Vaughan recognized this and he sent Washington a marble mantelpiece with bucolic scenes of rural life symbolically celebrating the General’s return to Mount Vernon.

  • Frieze (2 of 3)

    In 1785, George Washington hired John Rawlins, a plasterer from Annapolis, Maryland, to complete the decoration of the New Room. Rawlins continued the agricultural theme by depicting the tools of wheat farming in the composition ornament and plaster motifs of the room.  The central motif in the friezes above the doors contains a scythe, a wheat flail, and an urn surrounded by wheat, symbolizing Washington’s perception of himself as an American Cincinnatus and his vision for the agricultural future of the new nation.

  • Ceiling Decoration (3 of 3)

    Rawlins continued the motif into the elaborate plaster ceiling decoration with a shovel, sickles, and a sheaf of wheat.
