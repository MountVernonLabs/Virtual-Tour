• Cistern (1 of 2)

    Cisterns catch and store water, and they are either filled manually or by catching falling rainwater. Water-holding reservoirs were important to have in the gardens for watering plants in the summer, especially during times of drought. There is no evidence George Washington’s gardens had cisterns. The ones in the lower garden were added as a fanciful feature when the Mount Vernon Ladies’ Association restored the garden in 1937.
  • Cistern (2 of 2)

    In The Universal Gardener and Botanist, London 1778, Thomas Mawe states, “A water reservoir close to the garden was essential for watering plants in the summer, particularly when they were planted out in time of drought. It was also used for watering beds of young seedlings and plants in pots.”
