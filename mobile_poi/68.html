• The East Front of Mount Vernon (1 of 3)

    Edward Savage’s paintings of Mount Vernon’s east and west fronts are the earliest known views of Washington’s famed estate, painted between 1792 and 1795. The artist depicted the bucolic setting of the Mansion, its outbuildings, the brick ha-ha wall and wooden fence framing the expansive green of the East Lawn, and the Potomac River in the distance.

  • The House for Families (2 of 3)

    The two-story tan building to the right of the large tree is the House for Families, a structure that provided housing for the enslaved workforce before the completion of the greenhouse slave quarters in the early 1790s. Archaeological excavations of the House for Families site have provided rich information about the lives of the enslaved people at Mount Vernon.

  • Washington’s Deer Park (3 of 3)

    Following aristocratic British practice, George Washington fenced off 18 acres on the slope between the Mansion and the Potomac River to serve as “a paddock for deer” or deer park. Washington’s deer park served a picturesque function, providing his guests with the delightful spectacle of seemingly wild deer roaming through parkland. Washington’s friends sent him both English and American fawns for the park. The Washingtons treated them as family pets as they got older, even after the partly tame deer escaped from the park in 1792.
