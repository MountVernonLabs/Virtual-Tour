• Mill Furniture (1 of 3)

    All of the wooden pieces surrounding the millstones are referred to as mill furniture. The horse is a four-legged wooden frame that holds a tapered grain container called the hopper. Grain is poured into the hopper by the miller or an apprentice. Grain drops from the bottom of the hopper into the shoe, a wooden v-shaped funnel that feeds grain into the millstones. The damsel, a wooden shaft with iron staples, rotates with the runner stone and agitates against the shoe, causing grain to drop into the millstones.
  • Crook String (2 of 3)

    The leather strap, called a crook string, connects to the end of the shoe and allows the miller to adjust the height of the shoe and regulate the amount of grain entering the millstones. The crook string is adjusted with a twist peg located downstairs on the meal bin. Tightening or loosening the peg will raise or lower the shoe and adjust the feed rate to the millstones.
  • The Hoop (3 of 3)

    Surrounding the millstones is the casing, or hoop. The casing collects all the meal or flour exiting from the millstones and forces ground meal or flour into a small hole in the floor, dropping it down a chute into a bin on the lower floor.
