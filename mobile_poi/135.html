• Coat of Arms (1 of 4)

    As part of his 1757–1759 remodeling of Mount Vernon, George Washington installed a carved coat of arms at the top of the chimney wall in the west parlor. From this conspicuous location in the Mansion’s most formal entertaining space, the coat of arms served to proclaim his gentry status and family lineage.

    Image credit: Russ Flint
  • Coat of Arms (2 of 4)

    This coat of arms was probably carved by an unknown local woodcarver following a design supplied by Washington. The device features an oval shield with three stars above two horizontal bars. The crest atop the shield is a griffin, a mythological creature, with the body of a lion and the wings of an eagle or hawk. The slight asymmetry of the design exemplifies the rococo style.

    Image credit: Russ Flint
  • Coat of Arms (3 of 4)

    Washington also displayed the griffin crest on the cast iron fireback set into the back wall of the west parlor fireplace. This fireback was commissioned in Philadelphia in 1787, while the general was presiding over the Constitutional Convention. Its installation at Mount Vernon demonstrates Washington’s continued use of heraldic devices, even after the Revolutionary War had officially severed political ties with the British aristocracy that these emblems symbolized.

  • Coat of Arms as a Book Plate (4 of 4)

    Like many American colonists, Washington displayed a coat of arms that was historically associated with his family lineage, not one granted to him personally, as was the practice in Britain. In 1791, Sir Isaac Heard, of the College of Arms in London, sent Washington this small watercolor of the arms officially granted by Queen Elizabeth I in 1595 to the president’s ancestor, Sir Laurence Washington of Sulgrave Manor.
