• HMS Savage (1 of 3)

    In the spring of 1781, the British sloop-of-war HMS Savage anchored in the Potomac waters below Mount Vernon. The Savage, under the command of Captain Thomas Graves, had recently landed on the Maryland side of the river and had put to the torch several “gentleman’s houses” in sight of Mount Vernon. With Washington away in New York, Lund Washington agreed to supply the British man of war with requested foodstuffs, fearing the consequences. (Continued)

  • Mount Vernon Slaves Run Away (2 of 3)

    While the Savage was anchored in the waters near the Mansion, seventeen Mount Vernon slaves made their way to the ship, hoping to gain their freedom. The runaways included fourteen men and three women: Peter, Lewis, Frank, Frederick (an overseer), Gunner (a brick maker), Harry, Tom, Sambo, Thomas (a house servant), Peter, Stephen (a cooper), James Watty, Daniel, Lucy, Esther, and Deborah, who was sixteen years old. Seven of these escaped slaves were returned to Mount Vernon after the Siege of Yorktown in October 1781.

  • HMS Savage at Mount Vernon (3 of 3)

    When George Washington heard of Lund Washington’s accommodating behavior towards the British he was severely disappointed. In an April 30, 1781, letter Washington declared, that “which gives me most concern, is, that you should go on board the enemys Vessels, and furnish them with refreshments. . . It would have been a less painful circumstance to me, to have heard, that in consequence of your non-compliance with their request, they had burnt my House, and laid the Plantation in ruins.”
