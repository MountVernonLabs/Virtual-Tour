• Candle Stands (1 of 3)

    These matching candle stands are believed to be the “2 Candle Stands” listed in the New Room in the inventory taken after George Washington’s death. With a glittering silver or glass candelabrum perched on top of each, they would have provided an elegant display in the room. They are made of imported mahogany, with delicate scrolls and leaves carved out of the solid wood.

  • Candle Stands (2 of 3)

    Washington is known to have purchased candle stands in 1759 from James Allan, a Scottish-born cabinetmaker working near Fredericksburg, Virginia. The complicated construction and skillful carving suggests that these pieces were made by a craftsman trained in London, so perhaps Allan imported them or had a recently-arrived journeyman working in his shop.

  • Candle Stands (3 of 3)

    Washington’s candle stands are among the most high-style objects he acquired prior to the American Revolution. They exemplify the fashionable rococo style, which was popularized in England and its colonies by Thomas Chippendale’s pattern book, The Gentleman and Cabinet-Maker’s Director, first published in London in 1754. The rococo style is light, graceful, and somewhat exotic, with curving lines, foliage, shells, and other naturalistic elements.
