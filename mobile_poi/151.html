  • Martha Washington’s Chair Cushions (1 of 3)

    In 1766, Martha Washington acquired from London upholsterer Philip Bell the materials needed to create "one dozn. Chair bottoms." Over the next thirty-six years, she carefully cross-stitched an intricate scallop-shell design and used the finished product to create chair cushions, which may have been placed on Windsor chairs in the little parlor. The cushions in the room today are reproductions, stitched by dedicated volunteers, of originals in Mount Vernon’s collection.

  • A Cross-Stitched Scallop Shell (2 of 3)

    Martha Washington was known to be a talented and versatile needlewoman. The unusual shell pattern in her chair cushion would have been very stylish in the 1760s and is thought to be her own design. It is worked in x-shaped cross-stitches, requiring twice as many stitches and twice as much yarn as the single diagonal tent stitches commonly used for needlework seat upholstery.

  • Inscription (3 of 3)

    An 1823 inscription on the bottom of one of the cushions indicates that Martha Washington made this cushion for her oldest granddaughter, Eliza Parke Custis, in whose family it descended. Other cushions were treasured as relics and preserved by the descendants of Mrs. Washington’s other two granddaughters, Martha (Patty) Parke Custis Peter and Eleanor (Nelly) Parke Custis Lewis.

    Gift of Mr. and Mrs. Edmund Law Rogers Smith, 1948 [W-1469/A]
